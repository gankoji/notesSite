Being Productive Seems Hard

Yet, there have been myriad moments over the last two months when I've been so productive it was a bit scary. There were commonalities to those moments, and these have been reinforced by my latest reading, so I want to bring those into focus here and try to solidify them into habits going forward.

Focus is a Skill

This seems obvious, and it is at face value, but what's less obvious is what it takes to cultivate this skill. Active removal of distraction possibilities, an understanding of times and windows where you will be reachable/available to your coworkers/cohabitants, a plan for your time. These are just a few examples of what it takes to really settle down into a flow.

Distractions Need Not Be Electronic

Just now, as I was writing this paragraph, I got distracted by the structure of my notes. "Should I split this long note into more atomic bits? Where would I put the bits, since this is at the top level of my folder hierarchy? What about that article from last week that said linked graphs are no good for finding anything anyway, so we should just figure out how to keep things tidy the old school zettelkasten way?" Boom, 5 minutes gone.

This, I think, is a symptom of my own media and entertainment consumption over the last several years. I find it more and more difficult these days to achieve flow and really put in deep thought on a subject. On the flip side, as I recognize this difficulty, I also find it more and more difficult to let myself relax out of a flow, clinging to it like it might be the last one I ever have. This, of course, makes home life difficult. But more importantly, it raises the question of whether or not my worries are right.

So What's the Solution?

To be honest, I don't know for sure. What I do know, is that I have some tricks up my sleeve to give it a shot. First, scheduling focus time and actually sticking to it. This is the real hard part, since it's the first step on the list. 2 hours or so, twice a day, every day. Probably as important as the schedule and sticking to it, is making sure there is a reasonable break in between the sessions, and that it actually gets stuck to as well.

Next, the easier to get into but harder to habitualize, is having a rough plan or outline of the things I'm going to try to accomplish at each session, and then working through that plan.

Finally, the last bit will be to incoporate more of the 'consciously achieving flow' steps I outlined above: communicating schedules of availability (and lack thereof) with others, removing distraction possibilities and temptations, and getting down to business.
